Tree Species in Agroforestry

Gliricidia (Gliricidia sepium)

Overview: A fast-growing tree often used as a shade tree and for improving soil fertility.

Benefits: Fixes nitrogen in the soil, which helps other plants grow better. Its leaves can be used as mulch or fodder for animals.

Uses: Commonly planted in alley cropping systems and for soil erosion control.

Leucaena (Leucaena leucocephala)

Overview: Known for its fast growth and high nutritional value for animals.

Benefits: Improves soil fertility by fixing nitrogen and provides excellent fodder for livestock.

Uses: Ideal for silvopasture systems (integrating trees with pasture) and can also be used for soil erosion control.

Mango (Mangifera indica)

Overview: A popular fruit tree known for its sweet and juicy mangoes.

Benefits: Provides high-value fruit, shade, and can help improve soil structure.

Uses: Often grown in agroforestry systems to provide fruit and shade, making it suitable for various climates.

Avocado (Persea americana)

Overview: Produces nutrient-rich fruit and is valued for its health benefits.

Benefits: Adds value to the farm with its fruit and provides good canopy cover.

Uses: Suitable for mixed systems where fruit production is combined with other crops.

Almond (Prunus dulcis)

Overview: Produces edible nuts and is well-suited to dry climates.

Benefits: Nut production provides income, and the treeâ€™s leaves can be used for mulch.

Uses: Ideal for agroforestry systems in drier regions where nuts can be harvested for market.

Cashew (Anacardium occidentale)

Overview: Known for its nuts and cashew apples.

Benefits: Offers a valuable crop with both edible nuts and fruit. Its leaves and shells can be used for compost.

Uses: Often planted in tropical regions where it provides a profitable crop.

Teak (Tectona grandis)

Overview: A valuable hardwood tree known for its durable and high-quality timber.

Benefits: Provides timber for construction and furniture, and its leaves can be used as mulch.

Uses: Commonly used in agroforestry for timber production in tropical regions.

Eucalyptus (Eucalyptus spp.)

Overview: Fast-growing trees used for timber and fuelwood.

Benefits: Provides a quick return on investment with its wood and helps in controlling soil erosion.

Uses: Suitable for systems where fast-growing timber is needed, but care should be taken regarding its water use.

Mulberry (Morus spp.)

Overview: Known for its edible leaves and fruit.

Benefits: Provides shade and its leaves can be used as fodder for silkworms or livestock.

Uses: Often used in systems where shade is needed for crops or animals.

Moringa (Moringa oleifera)

Overview: A highly nutritious tree often called the "drumstick tree" for its edible pods.

Benefits: Provides high-nutrient leaves and pods and helps improve soil health.

Uses: Ideal for integrating with vegetable or fruit crops to provide both food and shade.

Acacia (Acacia spp.)

Overview: Fast-growing trees that can quickly cover bare soil.

Benefits: Improves soil fertility by fixing nitrogen and provides fast ground cover.

Uses: Useful for restoring degraded lands and in systems where rapid growth is needed.

Robinia (Robinia pseudoacacia)

Overview: Known for its ability to grow in poor soils and fix nitrogen.

Benefits: Provides quick cover and improves soil quality, though it can be invasive.

Uses: Good for erosion control and soil improvement in challenging environments.

Bamboo (Bambusa spp.)

Overview: Fast-growing grass-like plants that are technically trees.

Benefits: Provides a versatile product for building, crafts, and erosion control.

Uses: Suitable for systems where quick-growing, sustainable materials are needed.

Rubber Tree (Hevea brasiliensis)

Overview: Grown for its latex, which is used to make rubber.

Benefits: Provides a continuous income from latex tapping and contributes to shade.

Uses: Best for tropical agroforestry systems where latex production is viable.
